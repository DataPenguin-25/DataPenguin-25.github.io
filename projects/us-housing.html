US County-Level Housing and Employment Analysis

Identifying Profitable Rental Markets for Landlords and Investors

Role: Data Analyst Tools: Python (Pandas, NumPy, Scikit-learn, Statsmodels), Tableau, Folium Duration: Approximately 4 Weeks
View Tableau Storyboard → View GitHub Repository →

Project Overview

Landlords and property investors face a fundamental question when evaluating rental markets: where can reliable tenants be found, in markets with healthy demand and reasonable entry costs? Selecting the wrong market can result in high vacancy rates, tenants who struggle to meet rent obligations, or overpaying for properties in saturated markets.

This project was undertaken to provide landlords and investors with a data-driven framework for evaluating over 3,200 US counties and identifying which markets present the most attractive opportunities for rental property investment. The analysis integrates housing metrics from the American Community Survey with employment data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics to deliver actionable insights.

The Challenge

The analysis was designed to answer several critical questions for prospective investors:

  • Which counties offer favourable rent-to-income ratios?
  • How do vacancy rates vary across counties and regions?
  • What economic indicators most strongly predict rent levels?
  • Can counties be grouped into meaningful investment categories?
  • Which counties combine strong income, low unemployment, and reasonable rent?

Data Sources and Preparation

American Community Survey (ACS) 2022

The ACS dataset provided cross-sectional housing and socioeconomic data for 3,222 US counties, including median gross rent, median household income, median home value, total housing units, vacant housing units, rental vacancy rate, and state/county identifiers (FIPS codes). The 5-year estimates offer stability and coverage across all US counties, including rural areas where single-year estimates would be unreliable.

Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) LAUS

The BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics dataset provided monthly unemployment data at the county level from 1990 to present, including unemployment rate, employment level, labour force size, and monthly time series observations. This time-dependent data enabled analysis of labour market stability and economic trends across counties.

Data Preparation

The data preparation process addressed several quality issues. ACS data contained corrupted values (indicated by placeholder codes) which were identified and removed. The raw BLS file contained formatting inconsistencies including whitespace, tabs, and non-numeric characters which were stripped and cleaned. FIPS codes were standardised to 5-digit strings with leading zeros preserved to enable accurate merging. A rent-to-income ratio was calculated to assess tenant affordability.

After cleaning, the ACS dataset contained 3,211 valid county observations. The merged dataset with BLS unemployment data contained over 1.3 million monthly county-level observations spanning multiple decades.

Analytical Approach

The analysis proceeded in four distinct phases:

  • Phase 1: Exploratory Analysis — Examined relationships between rental prices and potential predictor variables through correlation analysis and visualisation
  • Phase 2: Regression Modelling — Quantified the predictive power of income and home values on rental prices using linear regression
  • Phase 3: Market Segmentation — Applied K-means clustering to segment counties into distinct market types
  • Phase 4: Time Series Analysis — Examined unemployment trends and labour market stability using decomposition and stationarity testing

Key Findings and Visualisations

A linear regression model was fitted with median household income as the predictor and median gross rent as the target variable. The results confirmed that income is a powerful predictor of county-level rental prices.

Three Distinct Market Clusters

K-means clustering was applied to segment US counties into distinct market types. All variables were standardised using z-score transformation prior to clustering. The elbow method indicated that three clusters provide meaningful segmentation without overfitting.

Cluster 0: Premium Markets (241 counties) — These are affluent metropolitan and suburban counties with high incomes (median $95,144), high rents (median $1,568), high property values, and tight rental markets (vacancy 6.9%). Tenants have strong ability to pay. Entry costs are high, but vacancy risk is low.

Cluster 1: Middle-Tier Markets (1,445 counties) — These counties represent the broad middle of the US housing market. Incomes (median $67,028) and rents (median $911) are moderate, property values are accessible, and vacancy rates (10.8%) are reasonable. They often present the most balanced opportunity for investors.

Cluster 2: Distressed and Rural Markets (1,525 counties) — These counties exhibit lower incomes (median $51,669), lower rents (median $724), lower property values, and significantly elevated vacancy rates averaging nearly 25%. Low entry costs may appear attractive, but high vacancy presents substantial risk.

Geographic Distribution

An interactive choropleth map was created using Folium to visualise the geographic distribution of rental vacancy rates across US counties. Clear regional patterns emerge, with high vacancy concentrated in the rural Midwest and South, whilst tight markets dominate coastal urban areas.

Time Series Analysis

To assess labour market stability, time series analysis was conducted on BLS unemployment data. Monthly unemployment rates were analysed from 1990 to present, revealing clear cyclical patterns corresponding to economic recessions.

The Augmented Dickey-Fuller test yielded a p-value of 0.35, indicating non-stationarity. First differencing was applied to achieve stationarity, confirming that the series is suitable for further time series modelling.

Summary of Findings

  • Income Predicts Rent: Median household income explains 63% of variance in county-level rents. Higher-income counties command higher rents, and tenants in these areas have greater capacity to meet rental obligations.
  • Vacancy Varies Dramatically by Region: Rental vacancy rates range from under 3% in tight coastal markets to over 60% in some rural counties. The Midwest and rural South exhibit high vacancy, whilst the Northeast and West Coast show tight markets.
  • Three Distinct Market Types Exist: US counties naturally segment into Premium (241 counties), Middle-Tier (1,445 counties), and Distressed (1,525 counties) categories based on income, rent, and vacancy characteristics.
  • Low Rent Does Not Equal Low Risk: Some low-rent counties have correspondingly low incomes, meaning tenants may struggle to pay even modest rents. The rent-to-income ratio provides a more complete picture than rent level alone.
  • Economic Stability Matters: Time series analysis reveals that county labour markets experience significant cyclical variation. Markets with historically volatile unemployment present higher tenant risk during economic downturns.

Recommendations

Based upon these findings, the following recommendations were developed for landlords and property investors:

  • Target Middle-Tier Markets for Balanced Returns: Cluster 1 counties offer the most attractive balance. Entry costs are accessible, tenant incomes are sufficient, and vacancy rates are manageable.
  • Use Income as a Primary Screening Criterion: Counties with higher incomes support higher rents and present lower collection risk. Income data is readily available and provides a quick method to filter potential markets.
  • Avoid High-Vacancy Markets Without Local Expertise: Distressed markets with vacancy rates exceeding 20% present substantial challenges. Unless an investor possesses local knowledge and tolerance for extended vacancy periods, these markets should be approached with caution.
  • Monitor Unemployment Trends: Historical unemployment data provides insight into economic stability. Markets with volatile or persistently elevated unemployment warrant additional scrutiny.

Technical Implementation

The project comprised six Python scripts following a structured analytical workflow:

  • Utilised pandas merge operations to integrate ACS and BLS datasets by FIPS code
  • Applied StandardScaler for z-score normalisation prior to K-means clustering
  • Implemented train/test splits (70/30) for regression validation
  • Created interactive choropleth maps using Folium with GeoJSON county boundaries
  • Conducted seasonal decomposition and Augmented Dickey-Fuller stationarity testing
  • Developed comprehensive Tableau storyboard for stakeholder presentation

Reflections

This project reinforced the value of combining statistical analysis with clear visualisation. The K-means clustering approach proved particularly effective for translating complex multivariate data into actionable market categories that investors can readily understand and apply.

The challenge of communicating technical findings to non-technical stakeholders drew upon my years of experience in client-facing roles, where I have consistently been recognised for explaining complex information with clarity and patience. Just as I once translated automotive diagnostics into plain language for customers at Rehbein Ford, this project required translating correlation coefficients and cluster centroids into practical investment guidance.

The integration of cross-sectional housing data with longitudinal unemployment data presented technical challenges, but the resulting framework provides a more comprehensive view of market conditions than either dataset could offer alone. Future enhancements could include multi-variable regression incorporating vacancy rate and population density, as well as predictive modelling for rent levels.
