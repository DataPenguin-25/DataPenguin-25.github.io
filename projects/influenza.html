Influenza Staffing Strategy

Developing a Data-Driven Staffing Strategy for Flu Season

Role: Data Analyst Tools: Tableau, Microsoft Excel Data Sources: CDC Influenza Mortality Data (2009 to 2017), U.S. Census Population Data

Project Overview

A United States medical staffing agency faced a recurring challenge: determining where and when to deploy temporary healthcare workers during influenza season to prevent staff shortages in hospitals and clinics. The consequences of inadequate staffing during peak flu periods can be severe, affecting patient care quality and placing undue strain upon permanent medical staff.

This project was undertaken to analyse CDC influenza mortality data and identify regional and seasonal trends that drive demand for medical personnel. The objective was to provide actionable, evidence-based recommendations that would enable the agency to allocate resources more effectively.

The Challenge

The staffing agency required answers to several critical questions: Which states experience the highest influenza-related mortality and therefore the greatest demand for additional medical staff? What seasonal patterns govern flu outbreaks, and how can staffing be timed accordingly? Which demographic populations are most vulnerable, and how does this affect resource allocation at the regional level?

Analytical Approach

The analysis began with the acquisition and integration of two primary data sources. I obtained CDC influenza mortality data spanning 2009 to 2017 and combined it with U.S. Census population data. The datasets were cleaned, validated, and merged by state to create a comprehensive analytical foundation.

Given the well-documented vulnerability of elderly populations to influenza complications, I focused particular attention on adults aged 65 and above. This demographic lens proved essential in identifying high-risk regions and informing targeted staffing recommendations.

I constructed interactive Tableau dashboards to visualise both geographic and seasonal patterns in influenza mortality. The geographic visualisations employed choropleth maps to highlight state-level mortality rates, whilst time series charts revealed the cyclical nature of flu season demand.

Key Findings and Visualisations

The analysis yielded several significant insights with direct implications for staffing strategy:

High-Risk States: The choropleth map above reveals that California, Texas, New York, Florida, and Pennsylvania experienced the highest absolute numbers of influenza deaths. These states, shown in the darkest shading, reflect both their large overall populations and substantial elderly demographics. The red circles indicate concentrated areas of mortality requiring priority staffing attention.

Demographic Correlation: A strong positive correlation (r ≈ 0.9) was identified between the size of a state's elderly population and the number of influenza-related deaths. This finding confirmed the importance of prioritising states with large populations of adults aged 65 and above.

Seasonal Patterns: The seasonal demand chart demonstrates that influenza mortality consistently peaks between December and March each year. Across all nine years analysed, deaths from flu reached approximately 7,000 to 10,000 per month during winter peaks, declining to around 5,000 during summer months. This predictable seasonality enables proactive staffing decisions.

Year-to-Year Variation: Whilst the seasonal pattern remained consistent, the chart reveals variation in peak severity between years. The 2009 season (shown in dark blue) exhibited notably higher mortality in early months, likely reflecting the H1N1 pandemic that year.

Recommendations

Based upon these findings, I presented the following strategic recommendations to the medical staffing agency:

Prioritise High-Risk States: Concentrate staffing resources in California, Texas, New York, Florida, and Pennsylvania, where demand is consistently highest as demonstrated in the geographic visualisation.

Deploy Seasonally: Position additional personnel in target regions from November through March to align with the established peak period for influenza activity, as clearly demonstrated in the seasonal demand chart.

Target Elderly Care Facilities: Focus specialised resources in regions with large elderly populations, particularly nursing homes and long-term care facilities where vulnerability is greatest.

Implement Dynamic Monitoring: Monitor CDC data on a weekly basis during flu season to enable rapid adjustments to staffing allocations as conditions evolve.

Future Enhancement: Expand the dataset to include more recent years and develop predictive forecasting tools to anticipate future flu season severity.

Reflections

This project exemplified the power of data visualisation in communicating complex patterns to stakeholders. The Tableau dashboards transformed raw mortality statistics into intuitive, interactive tools that enabled decision-makers to explore the data and understand the underlying trends without requiring technical expertise.

The work also reinforced my appreciation for the human impact of analytical work. Behind every data point was a real person and family affected by influenza. Knowing that the recommendations derived from this analysis could help ensure adequate medical staffing and improve patient outcomes made the work particularly meaningful.

My background in client-facing roles, where I regularly translated technical information into actionable guidance for diverse audiences, proved invaluable in presenting these findings in a manner that resonated with healthcare administrators and staffing coordinators alike.
